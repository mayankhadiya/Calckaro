Income Tax Slabs for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27)

The Government of India made the New tax regime the default from FY 2024-25 onwards, and the slabs were revised in Budget 2025 for FY 2025-26 (Assessment Year 2026-27). The New regime offers lower slab rates but removes most exemptions and deductions. The Old regime retains higher slab rates but allows a wide range of deductions under Chapter VI-A and Section 10.

Which regime should you choose?

For most salaried individuals in the ₹7L – ₹15L income bracket who don't claim large deductions, the New regime is usually better — the lower slab rates and ₹75,000 standard deduction outweigh the loss of most exemptions.

The Old regime wins when you have substantial deductions: a home loan (Section 24b up to ₹2L interest), large HRA in metro cities, full 80C utilization (₹1.5L), 80CCD(1B) NPS contribution (₹50,000), or a 80D family floater health insurance (up to ₹75,000 for senior citizen parents).

For FY 2025-26, taxpayers can switch between regimes only once during the year (at the time of filing ITR). So pick carefully and use our calculator above to compare both for your exact numbers.

Common deductions under Old Regime

  • Section 80C — Up to ₹1,50,000 (PPF, ELSS, LIC premium, EPF, home loan principal, tuition fees, NSC, ULIP)
  • Section 80D — Health insurance premium (₹25,000 self/family + ₹50,000 parents if senior citizens)
  • Section 80CCD(1B) — NPS contribution up to ₹50,000 additional
  • Section 80E — Education loan interest (no upper limit, 8 years)
  • Section 80G — Donations to specified funds and charities
  • Section 24(b) — Home loan interest up to ₹2,00,000
  • HRA Exemption — Section 10(13A) — minimum of actual HRA, rent paid minus 10% of salary, or 40%/50% of basic (metro/non-metro)
  • LTA — Section 10(5) — Leave Travel Allowance twice in 4 years

How Section 87A rebate works

Section 87A provides a rebate that reduces your tax liability to zero up to a certain income threshold. For FY 2025-26:

  • New regime: Residents with total income up to ₹7,00,000 — full rebate, tax = ₹0
  • Old regime: Residents with total income up to ₹5,00,000 — full rebate, tax = ₹0

The rebate amount is capped — meaning the rebate cannot exceed the actual tax liability before cess. So you effectively pay zero tax plus zero cess on incomes within these thresholds. After rebate, the 4% Health & Education Cess is also zero.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which tax regime is better for me?

The New regime is the default from FY 2024-25 and works best for most salaried individuals with limited deductions (under ₹2.5L of total 80C, 80D, HRA). The Old regime can be better if you claim large deductions including home loan interest and HRA in metro cities.

What is the new tax regime slab for FY 2025-26?

New regime FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27): 0-3L Nil, 3-7L 5%, 7-10L 10%, 10-12L 15%, 12-15L 20%, above 15L 30%. Standard deduction ₹75,000. Section 87A rebate makes income up to ₹7L effectively tax-free.

What is the old tax regime slab?

Old regime FY 2025-26: 0-2.5L Nil, 2.5-5L 5%, 5-10L 20%, above 10L 30%. Standard deduction ₹50,000. Section 87A rebate makes income up to ₹5L tax-free. Allows deductions under 80C, 80D, 80CCD(1B), HRA, LTA, home loan interest.

Is the new regime default?

Yes. From FY 2024-25 onwards, the New regime is the default for all taxpayers. To opt for the Old regime, salaried individuals must submit Form 10-IE online via the Income Tax portal before filing ITR.

What is Section 87A rebate?

Section 87A provides a rebate that reduces your tax liability to zero. Under the New regime FY 2025-26, residents with total income up to ₹7L get full rebate. Under Old regime, residents with total income up to ₹5L get full rebate.

Does this calculator include Health & Education Cess?

Yes. We add 4% Health & Education Cess on the calculated income tax, which is mandatory as per CBDT rules for both Old and New regimes.
